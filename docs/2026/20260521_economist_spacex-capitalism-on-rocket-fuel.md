# SpaceX is capitalism on rocket fuel

> Make what you will of Elon Musk, his rocketry firm is a marvel of free markets.

## Thesis

The IPO is "infinitely improbable" on two axes at once — _inspiring_ (more impossible-seeming engineering ahead) and _worrying_ (lossmaking outfit, Musk holds total control via dual-class shares). The Economist comes down on the side of admiration: SpaceX is what capitalism looks like at its most remarkable — risk-taking, competition, mobilization of resources that turn the improbable into reality.

## Why it matters now (the news peg)

- **May 20**: SpaceX filed the prospectus for an IPO of about **$75bn next month** — _the biggest listing in history_ (vs. Alibaba 2014, which raised roughly a third of that; NTT and Aramco were partial state privatisations, not comparable).
- **May 21**: Starship test launch aimed at the third unsolved problem — recovering the **second stage**, which has to survive ~1,500°C atmospheric re-entry.
- Possible day-one ranking: top-10 most valuable company globally, market cap up to ~**$2trn**; NASDAQ index inclusion two weeks later (forced flow into trackers and pensions).
- Possible side effect: Musk becomes **Earth's first trillionaire** — net worth comparable to all households in South Africa combined.

## Argument skeleton

1. **The engineering improbability ladder.** Liquid-fuel rocket to orbit (achieved 2008, 6 years after founding) → reusable booster landing upright (2015) → reusable second stage (the May 21 attempt). The piece frames SpaceX as serially solving things "no one has yet" done.
2. **Capitalism as the mechanism, not the obstacle.** Government policy helped get SpaceX off the ground, but Musk's drive solved problems the state could not. Concentrated wealth and trillion-dollar founders look in a populist age like _a failure_ of capitalism — the editorial reframes them as capitalism _working_.
3. **Spillover / option-value argument.** Even if Musk falls short of his stated vision, the launch fleet creates capacity that other capitalists will exploit. Historical analogies: Boeing → air mail with idle WW1 warplanes; Bezos → AWS from spare e-commerce capacity; Musk himself → Starlink originally absorbed unused Falcon 9 launch slots.
4. **Competition is real and intensifying.** Blue Origin's reusable rocket; Amazon's planned Starlink-rival constellation; China's LandSpace (Dec test launch) and Space Pioneer (April attempt) on reusable launchers.
5. **The financial risk side.**
   - Losing **>$1bn / month**, much of it on xAI ($8bn spent in Q1, still behind frontier labs).
   - Valuation up to **~100× annual revenue** (Tesla trades at 16×).
   - "Over 90%" of SpaceX's claimed **$28.5trn** addressable revenue is supposed to come from AI — roughly the size of US GDP today.
   - On the hook for the merged xAI and X (social network) — long Musk's "shareholder value but little cash" track record.
6. **Governance risk.** Dual-class shares with super-voting rights make Musk **unsackable**. Investors inherit his future business decisions _and_ his politics — explicit mention of likely backlash under a future Democratic administration over racism and DOGE-era damage under Trump.
7. **The "imperfect package" close.** Musk comes packaged with a "galactic ego and a cosmic appetite for risk" — parallel drawn to Henry Ford and J.P. Morgan. Capitalism's marvel is being able to harness imperfect people to do extraordinary things. Investors take the risk; the rest of humanity gets the spillover.

## Hooks for follow-up thinking

- The "data centres in space, solar-powered, no NIMBYs" pitch is the unproven core. Worth reading the companion briefing piece _Elon Musk is going all in on an unproven technology_ before forming a view.
- A dual-class IPO at this size, going straight into passive index funds, is a structural question for indexers, not just SpaceX investors — pension/tracker holders end up exposed without any governance lever.
- The Boeing / AWS / Starlink analogies are doing a lot of work in the argument. Each is a real example of byproduct → business, but the editorial doesn't ask how often the inverse happens (over-built capacity that never finds a second use). Worth interrogating before adopting the spillover thesis.
- The Ford / J.P. Morgan comparison is rhetorical cover for governance concentration. Both ended in regulatory or shareholder revolts. The piece doesn't engage that endgame.

## Update: 2026-05-22 — Flight 12 result

The May 21 launch was scrubbed (hydraulic pin on the chopstick arm). It flew on **May 22** — Flight 12, the first V3 Starship.

Net: **mixed but trending positive on the editorial's "improbable engineering" thesis.**

- **Upper stage worked.** Heat shield survived re-entry without burnthrough (the specific failure mode Musk had flagged); soft splashdown in the Indian Ocean on target after a 1h 6m flight.
- **Booster failed.** One Raptor Vacuum engine shut down early; Super Heavy aborted its boostback burn and was terminated over the Gulf of Mexico. The flight had only planned a soft splashdown (not a tower catch), so SpaceX absorbed the loss as acceptable for a V3 debut.
- **SpaceX called it successful; Musk called it "Epic"; Reuters more neutrally said "hit most targets".**
- **Market reaction muted.** Past Starship successes have averaged ~-1.3% for Tesla over 10 days — engineering wins are already priced in, which sharpens the editorial's "investors take the risk" line: the asymmetric exposure now leans toward downside surprise, not upside confirmation.

The cover's thesis ("capitalism is what produces this kind of engineering") holds up: the unsolved heat-shield problem moved from "unsolved" to "demonstrated once on this design." The governance and concentration worries in the second half of the editorial are unchanged.
