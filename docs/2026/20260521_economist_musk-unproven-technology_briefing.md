# Elon Musk is going all-in on an unproven technology

> What lies behind the world's biggest public offering.

This is the _Briefing_ that sits underneath the _Leaders_ cover [[20260521_economist_spacex_capitalism_on_rocket_fuel]] — the cover argues "SpaceX is capitalism working"; this piece does the fact-side stress test.

## The bet, in one line

SpaceX is filing a $75bn IPO that values the firm at ~$1.75trn (≈90× the $18.7bn 2025 revenue) on a thesis that **93% of its claimed $28.5trn TAM is AI**, mostly delivered via solar-powered data centres in orbit launched by a rocket — Starship — that still hasn't completed a second-stage reuse cycle.

For the bet to pay, three independent things must all work:

1. **Starship** must actually become routinely reusable end-to-end.
2. **Starlink** must keep compounding cash to fund the rest.
3. **Orbital data centres (ODCs)** must offer real advantages over a trillion-dollar terrestrial buildout that already has incumbents (Anthropic, OpenAI, hyperscalers).

If any of the three slips, the cash story breaks.

## Argument skeleton

### 1. The current dominance is real and asymmetric

SpaceX runs the world's largest space programme. Mike Grace (Longshot): _"owns the solar system right now, and the rest of us just rent it"_ — quoted to anchor that the dominance isn't a forecast, it's an existing fact pattern (90% of mass-to-orbit; >⅔ of all satellites in orbit; 10M+ Starlink customers).

### 2. Why an IPO now, after years of refusing one

Earlier posture (Gwynne Shotwell, 2018): _"We can't go public until we're flying regularly to Mars."_ The piece is direct about the reversal — capex quintupled to >$20bn in one year, xAI is bleeding $6.4bn/yr, even Starlink's $4.4bn operating profit can't cover the build. Public money is now the _only_ path to fund the AI-from-space bet at the speed Musk wants.

### 3. Starship: the operational chokepoint

- Falcon 9 already did half the revolution (booster reuse, mass-produced, 165 launches/yr).
- Starship is supposed to finish it: bigger payload (200 t), but mainly **reusable 2nd stage** — the unsolved engineering problem since the Space Shuttle.
- Booster recovery works (the "chopstick arms" on the launch tower). Second-stage recovery does not: splashdowns achieved, but heat shields chewed up. Musk's own words: _"not re-usable without a lot of work."_
- Slipped from a 2023–2024 takeover target. NASA's Artemis (humans-to-Moon) is waiting for a Starship variant.
- Aspirational cadence: same Starship flying multiple times _per day_ (vs Space Shuttle's weeks-to-months turnaround).

### 4. Starlink: the cash engine, with a ceiling

- Growth driver is the new **direct-to-cell (D2C)** product (no antenna needed; talks to ordinary phones) and the secretive government variant **Starshield**.
- $19.6bn EchoStar spectrum buy is a bet on D2C being a real business.
- Analyst split: Tim Farrar (TMF) thinks D2C is limited to places with no terrestrial masts (i.e. few people); Simon Potter (BryceTech) thinks it could go ubiquitous for cars, ag, logistics, military. Franco Granda (PitchBook) thinks launch + Starlink _alone_ can justify the $1.75trn — i.e. you don't have to believe the AI story to underwrite the IPO.
- **The bottleneck**: the planned "v3" satellites (20× capacity of current v2 Minis) only fit on Starship. So Starlink's growth ceiling is _tied to Starship working_.

### 5. ODCs: the actual unproven technology

- Not Musk's original idea — Starcloud flew a prototype on a Falcon 9 last year; Google plans tests in 2027 (reportedly contracting SpaceX to launch).
- Proponent case: 30% more sunlight than the sunniest Earth day, near-permanent sun in sun-synchronous orbits, no cooling overhead (~25% of terrestrial data-centre power goes to cooling), and political remoteness from NIMBY-driven data-centre bans.
- Musk: ODCs will be cheapest AI compute in 2–3 years. Sam Altman: _"ridiculous."_ That gap is the entire debate.
- The economics turn on three knobs: launch cost, sunlight-to-compute efficiency, and satellite unit cost. SpaceX wants to ride the Starlink learning curve into ODC mass production.

### 6. The Tom Mueller / Mars-cycle insight (this is the cleanest framing in the piece)

Mars windows open only every ~2 years. Hundreds of Starships built for Mars sit idle 25 months out of every Mars cycle. Mueller (SpaceX employee #1, now founder of Impulse): _"What do you do with the hundreds of Starships the other 25 months of the Mars cycle? Fly data centres to space, paid for by investors."_ This is the actual structural reason ODCs exist as a business plan — they're filling a fleet-utilisation gap, same trick Musk played using Starlink to fill Falcon 9 cadence.

### 7. Two ways SpaceX can profit from the AI bet

a. **xAI wins** — cheap orbital compute lets xAI overtake Anthropic / OpenAI.
b. **Industry-wide shift to space** — SpaceX becomes the Nvidia/TSMC of orbital compute, an indispensable infrastructure layer regardless of which lab wins.

Already a foreshadow of (b): Anthropic signed a deal on May 6 to pay **up to $1.25bn/month for 3 years** for compute at xAI's _terrestrial_ Colossus centre.

### 8. The risks the IPO buyer inherits

- Dual-tier share structure makes Musk effectively unremovable, regardless of execution.
- Track record of late projects (Tesla robotaxis, humanoid robots — both "just over the horizon for years").
- Early-VC overhang: lots of pre-IPO holders will want to sell quickly → likely volatility.
- 5-to-1 stock split lowers nominal price → courts retail "hopes and dreams" premium (D.A. Davidson's Gil Luria's phrase).
- Real competition from Blue Origin on launch, plus the broader question: _if AI capex slows, what happens to SpaceX's AI-dominated TAM?_

## The closer (worth keeping in mind)

Musk's recurring frame, paraphrased in the piece: nothing in the plan breaks the laws of physics, so it's "merely" an engineering problem — industrial and financial. The Economist's punch line: _"engineering is an unforgiving discipline."_

## Hooks for follow-up

- **Starship test result on May 21** is the single highest-information event for revising priors on this bet. The whole AI-from-space cash flow narrative routes through 2nd-stage reuse.
- **Anthropic ↔ xAI compute deal** is a strange shape — a frontier lab paying its direct competitor (xAI) for compute. Worth tracking whether this is desperation-for-GPUs or a hedged Musk-infrastructure call. Echoes the Microsoft↔OpenAI compute dependency.
- **Mueller's "25-month Mars cycle" framing** is the actual unit economics question: ODCs aren't competing with terrestrial data centres on first principles, they're competing with _Starships sitting idle_. That changes how you evaluate the ODC cost curve — opportunity cost of the rocket is near-zero, only operating cost matters.
- **Index-fund inclusion risk**: with NASDAQ inclusion forcing passive funds to buy at any price, governance concentration (dual-class shares) becomes a market-structure problem, not just a SpaceX-investor problem. Same critique as Meta/Alphabet but at much larger scale.
- **The Sam Altman "ridiculous" quote** is the single best short-vol marker. If ODCs become cost-competitive on a 2–3-year timeline as Musk claims, OpenAI's $1trn IPO planning looks under-priced for the wrong infrastructure stack.

## Update: 2026-05-22 — Flight 12 result

### What happened

- **May 21**: scrubbed. A hydraulic pin on the launch tower's chopstick arm failed to retract.
- **May 22**: launched. Flight 12 overall; the **first V3 Starship** ever to fly. SpaceX called it a success; Musk called it "Epic"; Reuters' phrasing was "hit most targets."

### Upper stage (Ship) — the key story

This was the thesis-critical part. The Briefing identified second-stage heat-shield damage as the unsolved blocker, citing Musk's own _"not re-usable without a lot of work."_ Result on Flight 12:

- Completed full controlled atmospheric reentry.
- **No heat-shield burnthrough.** Experimental leeward-side tiles survived peak heating and peak dynamic pressure.
- Subsonic banking maneuver → flip → two sea-level Raptor relight → on-target soft splashdown in the Indian Ocean. Total flight time 1h 6m.
- **Caveat**: this was a splashdown, _not_ a tower catch. The "Ship catch" milestone — the actual operational requirement for the "fly several times a day" cadence — still hasn't been attempted.

**Net**: the V3 thermal architecture is validated as a design. The full second-stage reuse loop is not yet closed.

### Booster (Super Heavy) — regressed

- One R-Vac (Raptor Vacuum) engine shut down early during ascent. Flight computer extended burns on the remaining five to reach the planned sub-orbital trajectory.
- The booster's **boostback burn anomaly** caused early mission termination over the Gulf of Mexico.
- Mitigating: this V3 debut was always planned as a soft splashdown for the booster, not a catch. So SpaceX absorbed it as acceptable risk for first-flight data.
- Still: booster _catch_ has been routine on Falcon 9 / V2 Starship; this V3 architecture has now demonstrated a regression that needs to be designed out before high cadence is possible.

### Market reaction

- Per Fortune (May 24), early investors continue framing Starship as the core of the $1.75–$2trn valuation.
- But empirically: previous Starship successes (IFT-9, 10, 11) produced no sustained upside for Tesla — average ~**-1.3% over 10 days**. The market treats engineering successes as already priced in.
- IPO valuation range has reportedly drifted from $1.75trn toward $2trn ahead of the offering — likely IPO mechanics / oversubscription, not Flight 12.
- **Implication for the asymmetric-risk concern in this Briefing**: positive engineering news doesn't generate excess return; negative engineering news (a future catch failure, a Ship burnthrough on a later flight, an ODC prototype that doesn't pencil) probably still does. The buyer of the IPO is short engineering vol with limited compensation.
